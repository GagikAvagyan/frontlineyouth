From Local Knowledge to National Practice

Peacebuilding Education in Schools

Since 2020, Frontline has been advancing peacebuilding education in Armenia by integrating research, curriculum development, and teacher professional development.

This initiative emerged from a simple observation: schools lacked systematic knowledge and practical teaching approaches related to conflict, peace, and peacebuilding. At the same time, ongoing education reforms and the introduction of new national curriculum standards created an opportunity to integrate these topics, particularly within social studies education.

In response, Frontline developed its own approach, grounded in Armenia's educational context and shaped through participatory discussions with teachers, students, and subject-matter experts. The result was the country's first comprehensive, locally developed peacebuilding education curriculum - designed not by adapting international models, but by responding to Armenia's specific context and educational needs.

As part of this process, Frontline developed three peacebuilding education handbooks for teachers. Each publication underwent expert review, classroom piloting, and revisions based on teachers' feedback before receiving official endorsement from the Ministry of Education, Science, Culture and Sport of the Republic of Armenia as supplementary educational resources.

The handbooks present peace not only as a topic of international relations, but also as a culture of everyday human relationships, human rights, democracy, cooperation, and responsible citizenship. They equip teachers with practical, age-appropriate, and safe methods for facilitating discussions on conflict, violence, peace, and social relationships in the classroom.

Today, the initiative continues through teacher professional development. More than 1,500 teachers from every region of Armenia have been trained using Frontline's peacebuilding education methodology and materials. Since 2025, in cooperation with the National Center for Educational Development and Innovation, peacebuilding education has also become part of the national online teacher training programme, making these resources accessible to educators across the country.

02

Youth Summer School Series

Seed the Change

Seed the Change is a five-day youth summer school series that brings together peacebuilding, environmental sustainability, and equality through non-formal education.

Each summer school is built around interactive learning, combining discussions, role-playing, experiential activities, outdoor excursions, creative methods, and community initiative design. Throughout the programme, participants use the "Seed the Change" Activist Handbook as a practical guide, workbook, and resource for continued engagement beyond the summer school.

Over five years, each edition has explored a unique thematic focus. Participants have designed and implemented community-based initiatives, created collages later featured in The Future We Want exhibition, explored conflict and trauma through art, and deepened their understanding of peace and conflict transformation. The most recent summer school was held in Georgia in partnership with international organizations.

The programme has also served as a foundation for several new Frontline initiatives, including the "Towards Trauma-Informed Education" educational toolkit and the emerging Frontline Peace Academy.

03

Participatory Research and Exhibition

The Future We Want

A creative summer-school exercise grew into a nationwide research initiative and a travelling public exhibition.

Research became a travelling public conversation.

The Future We Want initiative emerged from the collages created by young participants during the Seed the Change Summer School and the conversations they shared about the future. Together, they revealed an interesting contrast: young people often imagined the future with greater hope and possibility than the narratives they encounter in society and everyday life.

The collages became more than creative artworks - they served as expressions of young people's identities, perspectives, and aspirations. Building on this, Frontline launched a participatory research initiative involving diverse groups of young people, including school students, university students, employed and unemployed youth, young people displaced from Nagorno-Karabakh, and Yazidi girls. Through a series of focus group discussions, participants reflected on identity, belonging, gender, and their place in society.

The research findings were presented through an interactive art exhibition. The exhibition was displayed for a month in the Yerevan Metro before travelling to Ijevan, Vanadzor, and Gyumri in collaboration with local partners. In doing so, the initiative built upon Frontline's long-standing tradition of public exhibitions, reimagining it in a new format that reached wider and more diverse audiences.

The findings have also been compiled in The Future We Want publication, available in both digital and printed formats.

04

From Participatory Research to Educational Practice

Towards Trauma-Informed Education

This initiative explores how conversations about peace, conflict, and trauma can be academically grounded while remaining safe, accessible, and creative.

Throughout the research process, local and international education experts, teachers, and practitioners shared their experiences and approaches to trauma-informed teaching. These insights were combined with Frontline's field observations to develop a practical collection of trauma-informed educational methods tailored to the Armenian context.

The outcome is the Towards Trauma-Informed Education handbook, designed for educators across all subject areas. It offers practical, easy-to-use methods that help teachers create safer, more supportive, and trauma-sensitive learning environments while strengthening everyday communication and classroom relationships.

The handbook received a positive expert evaluation from the National Center for Educational Development and Innovation and was officially endorsed by the Ministry of Education, Science, Culture and Sport of the Republic of Armenia as a supplementary educational resource.

The initiative continues through practical implementation. Based on the handbook, Frontline has delivered teacher training programmes where participants apply the proposed methods in their own classrooms and provide feedback, contributing to the ongoing refinement and improvement of the resource.

05

An Animated Podcast and Interactive Learning Platform

PeaceON Podcast

PeaceON is an animated educational podcast designed to make complex social issues accessible, engaging, and relevant for young people.

Through storytelling, humor, and relatable conversations, the podcast explores the ideas that shape everyday life and encourages young audiences to think critically about the world around them.

Hosted by Piso ("cat" in Armenian), a curious animated cat, PeaceON transforms topics that are often perceived as difficult or abstract into engaging learning experiences. Across its episodes, the podcast explores themes such as media and information literacy, disinformation, financial literacy, environmental protection, fast fashion, social media, human rights, and active citizenship, using real-life examples and age-appropriate language.

In its latest seasons, Piso is joined by FYN, a skeptical companion who challenges assumptions, asks difficult questions, and encourages evidence-based thinking. Together, the two characters model curiosity, critical thinking, and constructive dialogue, demonstrating that questioning information is an essential part of responsible citizenship.

The learning experience extends beyond the podcast through the PeaceON mobile application, where young people can revisit episode content, explore additional learning materials, complete interactive quizzes, assess their knowledge, and earn certificates upon successful completion. By combining animation, gamification, and self-paced learning, the platform creates an engaging educational journey that continues long after each episode ends.
